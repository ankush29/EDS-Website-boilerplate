Bali Surf Camp

Seven days chasing the perfect wave along Bali's legendary breaks.

Surfing · Asia · 7 days

The alarm sounds at 5:30am. It's still dark outside the bamboo-walled guesthouse in Canggu, but the call of the ocean is louder than any reluctance. By 6am, we're paddling out into warm Indian Ocean water under a pastel sunrise, boards slicing silently through the still surface before the day crowds arrive.

Bali has been a surfer's paradise since the 1970s, when Australian shapers first discovered the machine-like perfection of Uluwatu's barrels. Half a century on, the island remains unrivalled for the variety and quality of its breaks — from the mellow, forgiving rollers of Kuta that welcomed generations of first-timers, to the hollow, consequential reef breaks of Padang Padang that have humbled many an expert.

Choosing Your Base

Canggu has displaced Kuta as the epicentre of Bali's surf culture. Its streets are lined with repair shops, smoothie cafés, and surf schools — a global community of wave hunters who all seem to have arrived one month ago and somehow never left. For our week, this would be home.

The Breaks

Echo Beach sits just minutes from the guesthouse — a long left-hander over sand that rewards patience with reliable, rideable surf from sunrise to sundown. On our second day, instructor Wayan — who has been surfing this break since he was eight years old — watches us from the shoulder, offering quiet corrections with the economy of a man who has said the same things a thousand times and means every word.

Midweek, we hire a driver and trek south to Old Man's on the Bukit Peninsula, where a right-hander peels languidly over a flat reef. It's more demanding than Echo Beach, but after three days of practice our pop-ups are sharper and our timing is finally clicking.

Beyond the Waves

Surfing takes the mornings. The afternoons belong to Bali itself. We wander through Seminyak's art galleries, eat exceptional nasi goreng from a roadside warung, and — on the penultimate evening — drive to Tanah Lot to watch the sun drop behind the iconic ocean temple as it has done for five hundred years.

It's the combination that makes Bali extraordinary: the waves are world-class, but the waves are only part of it. The island gives you ceremony and chaos, incense smoke and motorbike exhaust, a Hindu culture layered over a volcanic landscape that makes you feel, at every turn, that you're somewhere genuinely unlike anywhere else on Earth.

Practical Notes

Best time to visit

April–October (dry season). Swells are most consistent May–September from the Southern Ocean. Avoid the wet season (November–March) for surfing purposes.

Getting there

Fly into Ngurah Rai International Airport (DPS), Denpasar. Most major hubs have direct services. Book accommodation in Canggu — a 45-minute drive from the airport.

Fitness level

★★★ (3/5). Prior swimming competence essential. Basic surfing experience helpful but not required if booking lessons.

Surf schools

We recommend Odysseys Surf School (Canggu) for beginners through intermediate. Small group lessons (max 4 people). Book at least two weeks ahead in high season.

Stay

Desa Seni Eco Resort, Canggu. Yoga, spa, and surfboard hire all on site. Rooms from USD 90/night. A short walk to Echo Beach.

Cost

Budget USD 80–120/day including accommodation, food, lessons, and local transport. Bali rewards those who eat local.
