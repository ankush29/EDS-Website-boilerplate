The Summit Hike

Five days above the treeline in the heart of the Colorado Rockies.

Hiking · USA · 5 days

The air at 4200 metres is thin enough that a short scramble over loose talus has us stopping, hands on knees, watching our breath plume white against the blue. It's 8am. We've been climbing for three hours. The summit is still an hour above. None of us is complaining.

This is Colorado's Indian Peaks Wilderness — 74,000 acres of glaciated terrain, alpine lakes, and long ridgelines that stretch to the horizon in every direction without a road, a building, or a sign of anything built after the last Ice Age. For five days, this is our world.

Day 1 — Into the Wilderness

We begin at Brainard Lake Recreation Area (3200m), shouldering packs in the car park while a marmot watches us with studied indifference from a nearby boulder. The trail to our first camp at Long Lake is gentle — a deliberate choice on day one, allowing time to acclimatise before the serious altitude begins.

By afternoon we're camped at 3500m in a basin ringed by unnamed peaks. The silence is total until a thunderstorm builds over the Continental Divide and rolls through camp in a fury of rain and hail that has us in tents before dinner. This, too, is part of the deal.

Days 2–3 — The High Route

The middle days are the hardest and the best. We traverse the Pawnee Pass (3902m) under clear skies, looking north across a sea of summits to Rocky Mountain National Park. The descent to Pawnee Lake is steep and loose, requiring careful footwork and considerable patience.

Day three: an 8-hour traverse across the Continental Divide. The route takes us above 4000m for four continuous hours — high enough that the vegetation has reduced to a mosaic of lichen, tundra grass, and bare rock. The peaks feel both vast and intimate in the way that only high places do.

The Summit

Day four, before dawn, we leave camp for the summit push. Mount Toll at 4100m is a Class 2 scramble — no ropes required, but hands on rock for the final 200m of gain. The summit is the size of a dining table. We share it with nobody. The panorama takes in Wyoming to the north, New Mexico to the south, and the Great Plains stretching away to the east like a vast amber tide.

Practical Notes

Best time

July–September. Snow lingers on high passes until late June. Afternoon thunderstorms are routine July–August — plan to summit by noon. October brings early snow.

Permits

Wilderness permit required for overnight camping (free, quota-limited). Apply via recreation.gov 6 months ahead for popular routes. Walk-in permits available for unclaimed spots.

Fitness level

★★★★ (4/5). 10–15 miles per day with 600–1200m of gain. Prior backpacking experience recommended. Altitude acclimatisation essential — spend 2+ nights above 2500m before starting.

Getting there

Fly into Denver International (DEN). Brainard Lake is 90 minutes' drive on US-36 west. Car hire essential; no public transport to trailheads.

Gear

Four-season tent (hail and wind are frequent), 0°C sleeping bag, trekking poles, gaiters, rain gear, bear canister (required). Altitude medication (Diamox) worth discussing with your GP before travel.

Leave No Trace

Camp on durable surfaces, 60m from water. Pack out all waste. Fires prohibited above treeline. Respect wildlife — ground-nesting birds are vulnerable throughout summer.
